Teaching

I teach linguistics (general linguistics, syntax, and formal semantics) and English. As for teaching linguistics (especially in Graduation Seminar and other related classes), I encourage students to always have a "bigger picture".
That is, in addition to emphasising careful analysis of linguistic data, comparison of competing theoretical approaches, and the relationship between empirical observations and formal modeling (as usual in linguistics and any other science), I work with my students to not only focus on fitting themselves into some concrete research frameworks to form original research questions and improve their research skills, but also stick to the very basic idea that language is a window to the mind, which means that linguistics is ultimately a branch of science that seeks to elucidate the fundamental human nature. Having this agenda in mind, in my opinion, not only has a therapeutic value but also helps them connect a variety of research topics they are interested in (sometimes in a serendipitous way) and form deeper theoretical and empirical questions based on such interspersed but closely connected topics.
While rigossrous theoretical commitment is indispensable for productive research, I also encourage students to remain intellectually open to alternative analyses and constructive criticism. In my view, strong research develops not through abandoning one’s core ideas too quickly, but through refining them carefully in response to empirical evidence and scholarly discussion.
For these purposes, I consider close supervision and constructive discussion essential for graduate education. I believe that my educational role is thus primarily to create an ambience where students can freely discuss various academic questions with their colleagues and teachers, in which I frequently ask them what their “bigger pictures” are, so that I can remind them of the importance of sticking to the basics.
At the same time, I believe that this kind of intellectual activity necessarily presupposes constructive criticism. Since criticism can easily become oppressive when combined with asymmetrical power relations, I make a conscious effort, whenever I provide feedback or ask questions, to make my intentions explicit so that students clearly understand that my comments are not personal attacks or criticisms directed at them as individuals.
I also let my students do the same, trying to create an ambience where each participant can feel safe and be open to suggestions.

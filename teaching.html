Teaching

I teach linguistics (general linguistics, syntax, and formal semantics) and English. As for teaching linguistics (especially in Graduation Seminar and other related classes), I place strong emphasis on encouraging students to maintain a constant awareness of the “bigger picture.” That is, while careful observation of linguistic data and rigorous analysis are essential, I encourage students not merely to inherit existing theoretical frameworks uncritically, but rather to consider how their own analyses might account for other related phenomena-or even phenomena that at first glance may appear unrelated. At the same time, I value the fundamental idea that “language is a window into the mind”: namely, that linguistics is ultimately a discipline aimed at elucidating human nature itself. Approaching research with this perspective helps prevent one’s theoretical proposals from becoming unnecessarily complicated, leading to the expansion of its implications beyond its original domain.

Furthermore, while I emphasize the importance of openness to constructive criticism, I also believe it is equally important to preserve the originality of the core ideas underlying one’s theoretical proposals. I thus encourage students first to trust their own intuitions and attempt original analyses, and then to refine them carefully in response to empirical evidence, academic discussion, and constructive criticism. This is an approach I strive to embody both in my own research and teaching.

Based on these principles, I always aim to foster constructive discussions with students. Accordingly, I see my educational role primarily as creating an environment in which students feel safe to engage freely in academic discussion with their peers and instructors on a wide range of intellectual issues. In this context, I often ask students, “What was your bigger picture again?”, encouraging them to continually return to the fundamental questions underlying their work. Such intellectual activity necessarily presupposes constructive criticism. Since criticism can easily become oppressive when intertwined with power dynamics, I make a conscious effort, when giving feedback or asking questions, to clarify the intention behind my comments so that it is evident that they are not personal attacks or criticisms directed at the individual. I also work hard to cultivate an ambience where students themselves are encouraged to adopt the same attitude towards one another.
